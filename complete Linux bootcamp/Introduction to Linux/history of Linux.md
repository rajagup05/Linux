
## history of linux

Linux began in 1991 as a personal project by Finnish student Linus Torvalds. Frustrated by the high cost of Unix and the limitations of Minix, he set out to build a free, open-source operating system kernel for his personal computer.

### The GNU connection and Open source

- **The GNU Project**: Richard Stallman's GNU project had created many essential system tools but lacked a functional kernel. By combining the Linux kernel with GNU utilities, developers created a complete, fully functional operating system.
- **Going Open Source**: Initially, Linux code could not be used commercially. Following a 1992 transition, Linus relicensed the kernel under the GNU General Public License (GPL), guaranteeing the software would remain free and open to modification.

### Evolution and growth

- **Distributions**: The early 1990s saw the creation of distinct "distributions" (packages of the kernel, desktop environments, and utilities). Projects like Slackware (1993), Debian (1993), and Red Hat (1994) made installing and using Linux accessible to a wider audience.
- **The Penguin Mascot**: In 1996, the famous penguin mascot, "Tux," was introduced.
- **Corporate Adoption**: Throughout the late 1990s and 2000s, businesses increasingly adopted Linux servers due to their stability and security. The Linux Foundation was established in 2007 to support and standardize the ecosystem.


### Linux today


Today, Linux is the most widely used operating system in the world. While it has a smaller share on desktop computers, it forms the backbone of the internet, running the vast majority of web servers and cloud infrastructure. Additionally, the Android mobile operating system, powered by the Linux kernel, dominates the smartphone market.
